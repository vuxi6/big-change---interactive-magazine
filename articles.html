The Past

Articles that describe slower, more local ways of living and learning.

One notebook for an entire subject Education

In many classrooms, students worked with very limited printed material. A single notebook could hold almost everything they needed for a subject: definitions, solved exercises, diagrams and even exam preparation notes. The notebook became a personal archive that grew with each lesson.

Losing that notebook was not only an inconvenience; it meant losing months of effort. For this reason, students often treated their notebooks with great care, keeping them neatly organised and sometimes decorating them to show their importance. Learning was slower, but it was also more concentrated around a few key tools.

Key idea: Knowledge felt more “physical” and fragile, because it lived in a few objects students had to protect.

Waiting for news at the mailbox Communication

Before instant messaging, written letters carried both information and emotion. Families sometimes waited days or weeks to hear about exam results, job decisions or travel plans. Important letters were read slowly, sometimes aloud, so that the whole family could share the moment.

The simple act of walking to the mailbox could bring a mix of hope and nervousness. Messages were shorter, but readers often revisited them many times, keeping them as personal archives of important moments. The delay between sending and receiving created space for reflection, imagination and sometimes worry.

Key idea: Communication was slower, but each message received significant attention and care.

Shops that knew their customers by name City life

Local stores played a central role in daily routines. Shop owners often knew customers personally, remembered their usual orders and sometimes extended informal credit. Purchases could turn into short conversations about family, school or neighbourhood news.

This structure created a type of trust that was based not on digital records, but on repeated face-to-face interactions over time. A familiar shop could feel almost like an extension of home, especially for children who grew up visiting the same place for years.

Key idea: Daily life was deeply connected to specific places and familiar people.

Evenings without screens Home life

In many households, evenings meant conversation, radio, board games or reading. Families often gathered in the same room instead of spreading out across separate devices. Time passed more slowly, but there were fewer competing sounds and images.

While this did not automatically make life easier, it did shape a different rhythm: fewer rapid changes of focus and more time spent on a single task. People often remember those evenings as quiet but meaningful, connected more to voices and pages than to screens.

Key idea: Time at home was structured around a limited set of shared activities.

The Present

Articles about constant connectivity and the way it shapes study, relationships and cities.

Learning with many tabs open Education

A modern study session may involve several browser tabs, digital textbooks, recorded lessons and online exercises. Information is abundant, but attention is divided, especially when social platforms and entertainment are only one click away from school material.

Students have to develop strategies to stay focused: turning off notifications, scheduling short breaks or choosing one platform at a time instead of switching constantly. Teachers also adapt, combining traditional explanations with digital tools in a way that tries to support understanding without overwhelming students.

Key idea: The main challenge is not access to information, but managing distraction.

Group chats that never sleep Social life

Group chats can connect classmates, friends and relatives in real time, across different time zones. Messages, images and voice notes move quickly and rarely stop completely, especially in active school or family groups.

While this can create a sense of support, it can also cause pressure to reply immediately and difficulty disconnecting, even late at night. Muting conversations or setting limits becomes a personal decision that many people struggle with.

Key idea: Constant availability can blur the line between rest time and social time.

Invisible systems guiding movement Cities

Traffic lights, surveillance cameras and sensor networks now play a larger role in how people move through a city. These systems collect data to reduce congestion, improve safety or optimise public services such as lighting and waste collection.

Citizens may not think about these tools directly, but they experience their impact when a route is adjusted, a junction feels safer, or public transport becomes more reliable. Behind these changes are complex decisions about privacy, cost and priorities.

Key idea: Modern urban life is influenced by software and infrastructure that operate mostly in the background.

Balancing productivity and rest Daily routine

With study apps, task managers and calendar alerts, many people feel pressure to be constantly productive. At the same time, entertainment and social media are always within reach, making it easy to slip from focused work into long periods of scrolling.

Finding a healthy rhythm means deliberately setting limits on both work and leisure, instead of letting notifications decide the schedule. Simple habits, such as keeping the phone in another room during study time, can make a noticeable difference.

Key idea: Time management has become a central life skill in the digital age.

The Future

Articles that imagine more sustainable, calm and balanced ways of living with technology.

Cities designed for walking Urban design

Many future city plans emphasise short walking distances to schools, shops and parks. The idea is to reduce dependence on cars and rebuild a sense of neighbourhood, where basic needs can be reached without long commutes.

This type of planning can also make cities more inclusive, especially for children, older adults and people who do not drive. Streets become not only routes but also social spaces where people can meet safely and comfortably.

Key idea: Mobility is not only about speed, but also about comfort and access.

Classrooms that adjust to the learner Education

Future classrooms may combine human teaching with adaptive systems that suggest exercises, examples and explanations based on each student’s progress. Instead of everyone moving at exactly the same pace, some tasks could be personalised.

Teachers would still guide the learning process, but with more precise information about where students struggle or advance quickly. Technology, in this scenario, becomes a tool for fairness, helping to close gaps instead of widening them.

Key idea: Personalisation could make learning paths more fair and efficient.

Technology that invites pauses Well-being

Some designers imagine future apps that gently encourage breaks, limit unnecessary alerts and focus attention on one task at a time. Instead of competing for every second of attention, digital tools could be created with mental health in mind.

This might include default “quiet hours”, visual designs that avoid constant stimulation, and clearer options for disconnecting without losing important information.

Key idea: A healthier digital environment would help people protect their focus.

Carrying the past into the future Perspective

Even as tools and routines change, many values from earlier generations remain relevant: patience, community ties, and the ability to concentrate on one meaningful task. Remembering these qualities can guide how we design and use future technologies.

A thoughtful future would combine these older strengths with new possibilities, instead of replacing one completely with the other. In this way, progress becomes a conversation between times, not a break from what came before.

Key idea: Progress is strongest when it respects what came before.
